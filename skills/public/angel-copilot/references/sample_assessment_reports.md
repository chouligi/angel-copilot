## What the diligence sweep supports

The public story is directionally credible. Skyforge appears to be selling an enterprise AI workflow layer with customer-specific context, role-based deployment patterns, and governed execution across common internal workflows. Public launch materials support the claim that the company is moving from pilot-stage enablement into broader enterprise rollout, but the product page still uses language closer to “limited beta” than “fully generalized platform.” That does not invalidate the opportunity, but it does matter for how much of today’s revenue should be underwritten as software-like versus implementation-led. [Fictional source: S1]

The market need is real. Enterprises are spending on AI adoption, workflow redesign, and employee enablement, not just model access. That said, the budget line is crowded. Consultancies, LMS vendors, internal enablement teams, and horizontal AI suites are all competing to own the same transformation spend. This means demand exists, but competitive pressure is likely to stay high unless the company can prove measurable workflow ROI and durable account expansion. [Fictional source: S2]

There are also a few reconciliation items that should be resolved before underwriting the topline at face value. One source describes “$4.8M ARR run rate,” while another refers to “$7.2M qualified pipeline over the next two quarters.” Those may both be true, but they are not interchangeable and should not be blended. Likewise, “62 enterprise customers” may not mean 62 recurring paid logos if some of those deployments were partner-led cohorts or limited pilots. Finally, public messaging says “general availability soon,” while product marketing still points to restricted rollout. All three issues are fixable, but they affect how confidently traction should be scored today. [Fictional source: S1] [Fictional source: S3]

## Why those scores

### Team — 4.6/5
This is the strongest category. The founder-operator pairing is credible, execution velocity appears high, and the hiring plan is coherent for the stage. What keeps this from a top-decile score is concentration risk: too much of commercial momentum still appears founder-led, and repeatability beyond that motion is not yet fully proven.

### Market — 4.1/5
The category is real and urgent. Enterprises now spend not only on access to models, but on practical adoption and workflow change. The counterweight is that the category boundary is weak, and a large number of adjacent vendors can credibly pitch a similar transformation narrative.

### Product — 3.8/5
The product story is coherent and more specific than “another chatbot.” It appears to center on persistent workflow context, governed execution, and reusable internal patterns. That is promising. The main reason this does not score higher is that the product still looks partially beta-stage, and the line between product value and services support remains blurry.

### Traction — 4.0/5
Reported momentum is strong enough to take seriously. What holds the score back is not lack of growth but lack of clean definitions. I would want a bridge from contracted ARR to active paid logos, deployment type, seat expansion, and one-off implementation before scoring this as elite traction.

### Unit Economics — 3.7/5
Gross margin claims look attractive, but margin alone is not enough. The main question is delivery mix. A business can show healthy early economics while still being more services-dependent and less scalable than the headline suggests.

### Defensibility — 3.3/5
Moat is the weakest category today. If the code layer is easy to copy, defensibility has to come from embedded context, workflow ownership, governance trust, integrations, and data loops. I do not yet see proof that those effects are already compounding.

### Terms — 3.8/5
Valuation is investable if revenue quality is validated. The reason this does not score higher is that the investor package is still incomplete. A reasonable entry price does not substitute for clarity on pro-rata mechanics, information rights, or side-letter protection.

## Structural flags

The main structural issue is that the current investor-rights package is only partially known. Pro-rata rights are described as pending rather than clearly granted, and there is not yet enough detail on what minority investors receive in practice. For an investor who reserves follow-on capital selectively, this matters more than a small change in headline valuation.

The second structural flag is revenue composition. If a meaningful share of current revenue is tied to enablement, setup, or partner-led implementation, the company may deserve a different underwriting framework than a clean SaaS-style multiple would imply.

The third flag is go-to-market interpretation. “Customer” may refer to paid recurring logos, partner deployments, or limited-scope cohorts. That definition needs to be cleaned up before the traction narrative can be relied on.

## My fit call

For a high-risk angel profile using small starter checks and disciplined follow-on gates, this looks like a **plausible starter-check candidate**, but **not yet a reserve-commitment candidate**.

The reason is straightforward: this deal may be attractive on price, but it still lacks clean proof on two things that matter for selective follow-ons:

1. whether revenue quality is truly repeatable, and  
2. whether investor rights will be strong enough to support future participation.

That combination keeps the deal in **WAIT** rather than **INVEST**.

---

## Why not INVEST now

1. **Revenue-quality ambiguity** — recurring software versus enablement or one-off delivery is not yet disclosed cleanly.  
2. **Product-stage ambiguity** — public messaging implies both near-GA and limited beta.  
3. **Rights-package ambiguity** — pro-rata and information-right details are incomplete.

---

## What would move this to INVEST

1. A signed ARR bridge showing recurring revenue by product line and customer segment.  
2. Usage and retention data showing product depth that is not dependent on services intensity.  
3. Final legal docs confirming minority-investor rights in plain English.  
4. A clean definition of customer count: paid logos, recurring accounts, partner-led deployments, and pilots.

---

## Founder questions to send

1. Of current run-rate revenue, what percentage is recurring software, recurring enablement, one-off services, and partner passthrough?  
2. Of the reported customer count, how many are paid recurring logos today?  
3. For the top 20 accounts, how many are expanding product usage versus repurchasing implementation work?  
4. Which capabilities are GA today, which remain beta, and what share of revenue depends on each?  
5. What rights will minority investors receive in the final instrument?
